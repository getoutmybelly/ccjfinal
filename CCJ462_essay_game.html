Essay 1 — Recurring Victimization: Boost vs. Flag
Boost (State Dependence): The victimization event itself creates new vulnerability — offender learns your routine, trauma impairs judgment, locks stay broken.
Flag (Risk Heterogeneity): Pre-existing stable conditions (poverty, neighborhood, lifestyle) flag you as a target regardless of prior victimization.
(c) Both can operate simultaneously — someone flagged by poverty AND boosted after a burglary.
(d) Neither is universally better — empirical evidence supports both; context determines which dominates.
Essay 2 — Perceived Risk vs. Fear
Perceived risk = cognitive/rational estimate ("I think I might be victimized").
Fear = emotional/affective response ("I feel unsafe").
High risk, low fear: Cops, ER workers — they assess risk rationally but manage the emotion through experience.
High fear, low risk: Elderly woman in safe suburb — fear driven by vulnerability perception, not actual odds.
Related but not the same dimension — one is cognitive, one is affective.
Essay 3 — Criminological Theories + Opportunity
Strength of self-control theory: Explains impulsivity of both victims AND offenders.
Weakness: Tautological — low self-control explains everything, hard to falsify.
Value added beyond opportunity: Opportunity tells you where/when. Theory tells you WHY certain people put themselves there in the first place.
Example: Routine Activities explains the convergence. Self-control explains why someone is out at 2am in a high-crime area without a capable guardian.
